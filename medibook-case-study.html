Project overview

The challenge

A telemedicine startup had built a doctor booking platform but was experiencing a 64% drop-off rate during the booking flow. Users were starting the process and abandoning before confirming appointments. The business was losing patients at the most critical conversion point — not because they didn't want to book, but because the experience lost their trust.

04 — User Flow

Redesigning the booking
journey from 9 steps to 5

The original flow front-loaded all information collection before giving the user anything. I applied progressive disclosure — only asking for information at the moment it becomes relevant — to cut the flow nearly in half.

Before — 9 steps
1. Search doctors
2. Mandatory account creation
3. Choose doctor
4. Enter insurance details
5. Add GP referral info
6. Enter emergency contact
7. Pick time slot
8. Enter allergies & medical history
9. Confirm & pay
Strikethrough steps = eliminated or moved post-booking
vs
After — 5 steps
1. Search by symptom or specialty
2. View trusted doctor profile
3. Pick available time slot
4. Enter name + reason (2 fields only)
5. Confirm — receive video link
Additional info collected after appointment is confirmed
05 — Design Solutions

Key design decisions

🩺
Rich, trust-building doctor profiles
Each doctor's profile includes a short video introduction, a verified badge linked to medical board registration, plain-English specialty descriptions, and 3 genuine patient reviews — all before the user is asked to take any action.
📍
Persistent progress indicator
A "Step 2 of 5" bar is shown at the top of every screen in the booking flow. Users always know exactly where they are and how many steps remain — directly addressing the "I don't know when this ends" abandonment pattern.
🌍
Auto timezone detection
The calendar automatically displays all times in the user's detected local timezone, with a small tooltip confirming it. This eliminated the timezone confusion that was causing users to second-guess their selection.
👤
Guest booking — no forced account
Mandatory account creation before seeing available slots was removed entirely. Users book as a guest with just a name and reason. An account can optionally be created after the first appointment is completed.

06 — Testing & Iteration

Two rounds, two sets of
problems solved

I ran moderated usability tests with 6 participants via Lookback, using a Figma prototype. Each session covered 3 core tasks: find a doctor, book an appointment, and retrieve the video link after confirming.

✕ Round 1 — Issue found
Users missed the "Guest booking" option entirely. It was placed below the account creation form as a small secondary link — visually hidden from most participants.
✓ Fix applied
Moved guest booking above the account creation form and gave it equal visual weight. Guest bookings in testing rose from 20% to 71% of participants choosing that path.
✕ Round 1 — Issue found
The confirmation screen felt too plain — 2 of 6 users asked "Did it actually book?" and tried tapping the confirm button again, generating duplicate bookings in the test environment.
✓ Fix applied
Added an animated confirmation checkmark, prominent doctor name and time, and an "Add to calendar" button. Zero duplicate booking attempts occurred in round 2 testing.

08 — Key Learnings

What this project
taught me

01
Trust must be earned before you ask for anything
In healthcare especially, users will not share personal information with a platform they don't yet trust. The single most impactful design decision in this project was the doctor's video introduction — it converted a name on a screen into a real, qualified human being. A great profile page does more for conversion than a streamlined checkout flow.
02
Progressive disclosure is one of the highest-leverage UX tools
Asking for information only at the moment it becomes relevant cut the booking flow from 9 steps to 5, and reduced completion time by more than half. Every extra form field is a potential exit. The guiding question throughout ideation was: "Does the user need to give this now, or can it wait?"
03
The confirmation screen is not the end of the experience
A plain "booking confirmed" page left users anxious and uncertain. The confirmation screen is a critical moment of emotional resolution — users need to feel certain, reassured, and equipped with what comes next. Fixing the confirmation screen eliminated all duplicate booking attempts in round 2 testing without changing anything else in the flow.
